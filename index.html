How Super Mario 64 got 3D Platforming Right the First Time

Author: 9th Grade ItsMaximum

In 1996, the entire landscape of gaming was completely different than it is today. Most video games were displayed in two dimensions, but three-dimensional polygon graphics were becoming more mainstream. People had been exposed to pseudo-3D with games like Doom, but true three-dimensional graphics were failing to impress. The fifth generation of consoles started three years back, and several different companies were trying to figure out how to transition into a whole different dimension. With the upcoming release of the Nintendo 64 in September, all eyes were set on Mario, the Italian plumber who was about to make the debut into 3D. Super Mario 64 was set to be a platforming game, like its many 2D predecessors. A platforming video game is described as a subgenre of action game that involves jumping from platform to platform while avoiding any obstacles. The traditional game design formula obviously had to change, as square tiles wouldn’t work well in a 3D game. Also, short levels with only a single objective would not translate well at all. Furthermore, the simple “run and jump” movement was just not going to cut it. Nintendo had to figure out how to keep everything simple and easy to follow, but they had no other games to reference in the game’s creation. They had to essentially “invent” the game’s mechanics; they had to lay out the framework. In this video game world, Bowser has taken over the castle of Princess Peach once again. It is Mario’s job to collect power stars spread out through the whole castle. Through the 15 unique stages and the various secrets hidden throughout the castle, there are a whole 120 stars to collect. However, choices are given to the player, as only 70 are required to ascend the infinite staircase and enter Bowser’s final stage. Every corner of the castle is littered with things to be discovered, and exploration is highly encouraged. On top of the six normal stars available in each of the castle’s stages, there is another star awarded for the collection of 100 coins, which provides a benefit for looking in every nook and cranny. Though most parts of the castle require a certain amount of stars to access, there is no requirement for any specific stars a player must collect. This makes the game feel open and free, as the only thing one is “forced” to do is to beat bowser’s three hideouts. Simply put, Mario perfectly transitioned from a mostly linear game design to an open game design while keeping the same, refreshing feel of a Mario platformer. For Super Mario 64, many game mechanics were added or refined in a very positive manner. One of the biggest changes had to have been the camera. In the two-dimensional realm, the camera, or visible picture, only had to move left and right on a fixed line. Now, the camera had to passively move around Mario, and it also had to accept user input through the C and R buttons. Without the camera being fixed to Mario’s head in a first-person fashion, keeping the camera in the right spot, out of walls and entities, was a definite challenge. On top of this, this is a 3D camera being controlled by buttons, rather than an analog stick, so the camera is moved heavily by each press. In the end, the choppy camera complemented the movement of the game well, but a smoother and smarter perspective would have been beneficial to the game’s feel. Nintendo jammed a lot of movement features into one analog stick and three buttons. Players will generally start out with the simple run and jump, but the game will eventually teach different concepts through signs and discovery. There are long jumps, backflips, side flips, dives, triple jumps, and kicks, just to list a few. This plethora of options giver a player complete freedom to complete an objective in whatever way they want to. It makes unintentional maneuvers feel like a part of the game, rather than something that takes away from the experience. “Speedrunning” is a competitive aspect of playing video games, with the goal of completing them as fast as possible given a certain set of rules. Some categories may ban certain glitches, require 100% of the game to be completed, but others give the player free reign. Because of the skillful and scalable movement found in Super Mario 64, the speedrunning community for the game is one of the largest, and it has caused the game to rise in popularity in recent years. As stated before, 70 stars are normally required to beat the game, but the “Any%” category for Super Mario 64 requires a whopping 0 stars; “Any%” generally incurs beating the game as fast as possible, by any means necessary. This is possible due to an oversight in the game allowing a player’s backwards momentum to never be capped. Backwards long-jumping, or “BLJ” for short, allows a player can build up enough speed to phase through objects. The specifics of the BLJ glitch is complicated, but speedrunners can build up massive amounts of speed on different staircases to skip the star requirements for all three Bowser stages. Though Super Mario 64 can be completed in a very short amount of time using unintentional glitches, the most popular category is actually “70 Star”, which bans the use of this exploit. This may seem unusual at first, but this categories’ popularity shows just how enjoyable people find the game’s movement. The release of the Nintendo 64 classic is imminent, considering the bewildering success of the previous miniature remakes of Nintendo’s NES and SNES, which released before the Nintendo 64. These contained several of their respective console’s greatest hits. A release of the game on a future N64 classic is imminent, considering its legacy and perfection. This game will likely be one of the major driving forces behind N64 classic sales, and it will be a great way to play through the game again on a modern television When Super Mario 64 was released, it received worldwide acclaim. Even with all the odds stacked against them, Nintendo managed to get nearly everything right the first time. The movement was crisp, and the environment was open and creative. Despite the Nintendo 64 controller having the odd design that it did, every button press, and finger movement felt so natural. There were a few hiccups when it came to graphics and the camera, but the pros far outshone the cons. Put simply, Nintendo nailed the formula for a successful 3D platformer the first time. Whether it be on original hardware, an emulator, or the upcoming N64 classic, Super Mario 64 is a definite buy so many years later.
